# Research Report

## Executive Summary

The emergence of rapid-acting antidepressant interventions has fundamentally transformed our understanding of neural plasticity as a mechanistic substrate for therapeutic efficacy in psychiatric treatment. Traditional conceptualizations of antidepressant action emphasized gradual monoaminergic modulation and synaptic scaling over weeks to months, yet the clinical observation that interventions such as electroconvulsive therapy (ECT), repetitive transcranial magnetic stimulation (rTMS), and serotonergic psychedelics produce robust mood improvements within hours to days has catalyzed a paradigm shift toward examining structural plasticity mechanisms. A comprehensive analysis of these three neuromodulatory approaches reveals distinct yet overlapping mechanisms of synaptic rewiring, with rTMS demonstrating particular precision in its capacity to induce circuit-specific structural modifications ([Liao et al. 2025](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41583-024-00876-0)).

The mechanistic substrate underlying rTMS therapeutic efficacy encompasses not merely transient electrophysiological effects but also enduring structural modifications at the synaptic level. Emerging evidence indicates that rTMS facilitates spinogenesis—the de novo formation of dendritic spines—throughout targeted cortical regions and connected neural circuits, with notable effects on spine morphology including increases in spine head diameter and neck length that correlate with enhanced synaptic strength ([Sharbafshaaer et al. 2024](https://doi.org/10.3390/biomedicines12112506)). Critically, rTMS distinguishes itself among neuromodulatory interventions through its capacity to induce directional plasticity, enabling clinicians to selectively potentiate or suppress cortical excitability with remarkable temporal and spatial precision through manipulation of stimulation parameters such as frequency, intensity, and pattern ([Antal et al. 2022](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cnp.2022.05.002)). This directional control extends to circuit-specific rewiring, whereby rTMS can preferentially modulate particular prefrontal-subcortical pathways implicated in mood regulation, a level of specificity not achievable with ECT's global seizure-induced plasticity.

The molecular mechanisms mediating rTMS-induced synaptic refinement involve sophisticated neurotrophin signaling cascades, particularly the balance between proBDNF and mature BDNF, which functions as a fundamental molecular neural switch governing whether circuits undergo pruning or consolidation. ProBDNF, secreted as a precursor molecule requiring proteolytic processing by matrix metalloproteinase-9 (MMP-9) and tissue plasminogen activator (tPA) to generate mature BDNF capable of TrkB receptor activation, promotes synaptic refinement through p75NTR-mediated signaling, while mature BDNF supports spine stabilization and growth ([Kirkovski et al. 2023](https://doi.org/10.1007/s00429-023-02634-x)). In contrast, ECT-mediated neural plasticity appears to operate through global seizure-induced mechanisms that trigger widespread structural reorganization across distributed neural networks, potentially involving more diffuse proBDNF signaling shifts 00138-x). Psychedelic-induced structural plasticity, mediated primarily through 5-HT2A receptor activation on cortical pyramidal neurons, promotes dendritic remodeling and spinogenesis through distinct Gq-coupled signaling pathways, though the specificity of this rewiring appears less circuit-directed compared to rTMS ([Nichols 2016](https://doi.org/10.1124/pr.115.011478)). The recognition that these differential rewiring mechanisms exist represents a paradigm shift toward circuit-informed treatment personalization, whereby the precision of rTMS-induced synaptic rewiring may offer particular advantages for patients whose depression manifests through specific prefrontal circuit dysfunction.

## Comparative Mechanisms of Synaptic Rewiring in Neuromodulation: rTMS, ECT, and Psychedelics

The emergence of rapid-acting antidepressant interventions has fundamentally transformed our understanding of neural plasticity as a mechanistic substrate for therapeutic efficacy in psychiatric treatment. Traditional conceptualizations of antidepressant action emphasized gradual monoaminergic modulation and synaptic scaling over weeks to months, yet the clinical observation that interventions such as electroconvulsive therapy (ECT), ketamine, and certain psychedelic compounds produce robust mood improvement within hours to days necessitated a paradigm shift toward examining faster, structurally mediated plasticity mechanisms. This realization has prompted systematic investigation into how distinct neuromodulation approaches engage overlapping or divergent molecular and cellular pathways to achieve lasting changes in neural circuit function.

A recent comprehensive review by Liao, Dua, Wojtasiewicz, Liston, and Kwan synthesized the emerging literature on structural neural plasticity evoked by rapid-acting antidepressant interventions, providing a critical framework for understanding how diverse treatment modalities converge upon common synaptic remodeling processes ([Liao et al. 2025](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41583-024-00876-0)). Their analysis highlights that while electroconvulsive therapy, repetitive transcranial magnetic stimulation (rTMS), and psychedelic compounds all appear to promote dendritic spine formation, axonal sprouting, and hippocampal volume changes, the temporal dynamics, anatomical specificity, and underlying signaling cascades differ substantially across modalities. This heterogeneity poses a fundamental challenge for the field: distinguishing mechanism-specific effects from epiphenomena of general arousal or seizure activity becomes essential for rational treatment optimization and biomarker development.

The differentiation of rTMS-specific mechanisms from broader neuromodulation approaches assumes particular importance given the clinical utility of rTMS as a non-invasive intervention with relatively focal targeting capabilities. Unlike ECT, which induces generalized seizures and systemic autonomic activation, rTMS delivers magnetic fields that directly excite cortical neurons in a spatially restricted manner, Putative mechanisms have focused on the induction of long-term potentiation-like plasticity at cortico-cortical and cortico-subcortical synapses, with downstream effects on distributed mood-regulatory networks. However, the extent to which rTMS engages the same structural plasticity pathways documented in ECT and psychedelic-induced neural remodeling remains incompletely characterized, and the reliance on indirect indicators such as functional connectivity changes or behavioral correlates has limited mechanistic precision. Establishing whether rTMS induces comparable spine density alterations, neurotrophin expression shifts, or microglial-dependent synaptic pruning compared to rapid-acting pharmacological interventions will be critical for positioning rTMS within the broader landscape of plasticity-based therapeutics and for identifying predictors of treatment response across patient populations.

## rTMS-Induced Synaptic Rewiring: Spinogenesis, Spine Morphology, and Directional Plasticity

rTMS-Induced Synaptic Rewiring: Spinogenesis, Spine Morphology, and Directional Plasticity

The mechanistic substrate underlying the therapeutic efficacy of repetitive transcranial magnetic stimulation encompasses not merely transient electrophysiological effects but also enduring structural modifications at the synaptic level. Emerging evidence indicates that rTMS facilitates spinogenesis—the de novo formation of dendritic spines—throughout targeted cortical regions and connected neural circuits, thereby reorganizing synaptic architecture in a manner that outlasts the immediate stimulation period ([Liao et al. 2025](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41583-024-00876-0)). This structural remodeling represents a critical component of rTMS-induced neuroplasticity, as dendritic spines serve as the primary postsynaptic sites of excitatory glutamatergic transmission and their morphological characteristics directly correlate with synaptic strength and functional connectivity.

The induction of spinogenesis by rTMS operates through multiple intersecting molecular pathways. High-frequency stimulation paradigms and theta burst stimulation protocols have been demonstrated to upregulate brain-derived neurotrophic factor expression, with elevated serum BDNF levels observed in clinical populations following repeated rTMS sessions ([Pan et al. 2023](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jad.2022.11.059)). The neurotrophic hypothesis of rTMS action posits that activity-dependent BDNF release TrkB receptor activation, subsequently triggering intracellular signaling cascades that promote dendritic branching and spine formation. Notably, individual genetic variation in BDNF polymorphisms influences the magnitude of rTMS-induced behavioral effects, suggesting that neurotrophic signaling constitutes a limiting factor in stimulation-evoked structural plasticity ([Chang et al. 2014](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.brs.2014.03.008)). This gene-stimulus interaction may account for the considerable interindividual variability in clinical response that characterizes rTMS therapy.

The morphological remodeling of existing spines represents an additional dimension of rTMS-mediated structural plasticity beyond de novo spinogenesis. Dendritic spines are heterogeneous structures categorized into stubby, thin, and mushroom morphologies, each possessing distinct functional properties. Thin spines, characterized by elongated necks and small heads, represent learning-related plastic structures capable of undergoing activity-dependent enlargement into mature mushroom spines, which possess large heads and short necks associated with stable memory traces. The balance between proBDNF and mature BDNF signaling critically regulates this morphological transformation, as proBDNF preferentially binds to p75NTR receptors to promote spine pruning while mature BDNF TrkB signaling supports spine stabilization and growth ([Liao et al. 2025](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41583-024-00876-0)). rTMS appears to shift this equilibrium toward mature BDNF predominance, thereby favoring the formation and stabilization of mushroom-type spines that consolidate synaptic modifications.

Directional plasticity—the capacity for rTMS to selectively potentiate or depress synaptic strength depending on stimulation parameters—further underscores the sophistication of activity-dependent structural remodeling. Continuous theta burst stimulation has been shown to enhance neurogenesis in the subventricular zone and support neuronal survival following ischemic injury, suggesting that different stimulation protocols may recruit distinct downstream effectors to achieve complementary structural outcomes ([Zong et al. 2022](https://doi.org/10.7150/thno.71832)). Similarly, intermittent theta burst stimulation applied to cerebellar regions facilitates neurological recovery through the cAMP/PKA/CREB pathway, indicating that the molecular machinery governing gene transcription contributes to long-lasting structural changes in synaptic architecture ([Yang et al. 2025](https://doi.org/10.1161/STROKEAHA.124.048697)). These findings collectively demonstrate that rTMS-induced spinogenesis is not a uniform process but rather a parameterized response shaped by stimulation frequency, pattern, intensity, and target region.

The neuroinflammatory milieu within which spinogenesis occurs exerts profound influences on the efficacy and specificity of rTMS-evoked structural remodeling. Neuroinflammation, characterized by microglial activation and proinflammatory cytokine release, can either facilitate or impede synaptic plasticity depending on contextual factors. rTMS has been shown to exert anti-inflammatory effects in pathological states, with modulation of neuroinflammation representing a proposed mechanism through which stimulation may restore circuit function in conditions such as neuropathic pain ([Bai et al. 2023](https://doi.org/10.3389/fimmu.2023.1172293)). The interplay between rTMS, neuroinflammation, and spinogenesis suggests that the therapeutic relevance of structural plasticity may depend critically on the baseline inflammatory state of the target tissue. In healthy neural circuits, moderate inflammatory signaling may support adaptive spine remodeling, whereas excessive neuroinflammation may trigger spine loss and circuit dysfunction that rTMS must counteract to achieve clinical benefit.

The integration of rTMS with complementary neuromodulatory approaches may potentiate spinogenic effects beyond what stimulation alone achieves. Aerobic exercise, which independently promotes neurotrophic factor expression and spinogenesis through activity-dependent mechanisms, has been proposed as a synergistic intervention capable of enhancing rTMS-induced structural plasticity ([Hendrikse et al. 2017](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.neubiorev.2017.09.023)). The convergence of exercise-evoked and rTMS-evoked neurotrophic signaling on common downstream effectors suggests additive or multiplicative effects on spine density and morphological maturation, though systematic investigation of combination protocols remains limited. Quadripulse stimulation represents another parameter optimization strategy that may enhance the specificity and durability of rTMS-induced structural changes, though its effects on spinogenesis require further characterization ([Matsumoto and Ugawa 2020](https://doi.org/10.1007/s00221-020-05788-w)).

Methodological considerations impose significant constraints on current understanding of rTMS-mediated spinogenesis. The majority of evidence derives from preclinical animal models employing histomorphometric analyses of fixed tissue, which cannot capture the dynamic nature of spine remodeling in living human brain. Translational gaps between rodent and human cortical biology, differences in stimulation intensity scaling, and the impossibility of direct electrophysiological validation in human studies collectively limit confidence in the generalizability of mechanistic findings. Advanced neuroimaging approaches, including longitudinal diffusion tensor imaging and emerging quantitative susceptibility mapping, may eventually permit non-invasive assessment of structural connectivity changes in human subjects, though these techniques currently lack the resolution to resolve individual dendritic spines. Future investigation employing optogenetic manipulation of specific neuronal populations in combination with rTMS may elucidate the cell-type specificity of spinogenic responses and clarify how stimulation-evoked activity patterns propagate through distributed cortical and subcortical networks to produce widespread structural modifications.

## ECT-Mediated Neural Plasticity: Global Rewiring Versus Targeted Circuit Modulation

The mechanism by which electroconvulsive therapy exerts its therapeutic effects in treatment-resistant depression has been conceptualized through multiple theoretical frameworks, with the seizure itself representing the critical neurobiological trigger for downstream plastic changes. Post and colleagues provided early comparative evidence examining seizure models and the anticonvulsant properties of both ECT and repetitive transcranial magnetic stimulation, establishing a foundation for understanding how these divergent stimulation modalities influence neural excitability and plasticity 00138-x). The induction of a generalized seizure represents a qualitatively distinct neurophysiological event compared to the focal, non-convulsive cortical activation produced by rTMS, with fundamental implications for the spatial distribution and magnitude of subsequent plastic adaptations.

Seizure-dependent global changes constitute a fundamental component of ECT's mechanism, as the induced generalized seizure initiates widespread neurophysiological alterations affecting multiple cortical and subcortical regions simultaneously. This global seizure activity triggers cascades of molecular and cellular events that extend far beyond the immediate stimulation site, including widespread alterations in neurotransmission, gene expression, and cellular signaling. The anticonvulsant effects observed in seizure models following ECT administration suggest that the therapeutic mechanism may involve adaptive changes in neuronal excitability thresholds, potentially resetting pathological circuits underlying depressive pathophysiology 00138-x). These seizure-dependent changes contrast sharply with the more spatially restricted effects of rTMS, which produces effects primarily within the targeted cortical region and its immediate connected networks.

Neurotrophic factor release, particularly brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF), has been proposed as a critical mediator of ECT-induced neural plasticity, with seizure activity potently stimulating the expression and release of these growth-promoting molecules. The global nature of the ECT-induced seizure ensures widespread neurotrophic factor signaling throughout limbic and cortical networks implicated in mood regulation. This contrasts with rTMS, which may stimulate neurotrophic factor release in a more regionally selective manner depending on the stimulation target and parameters employed. The magnitude and distribution of neurotrophic factor release may therefore constitute a key mechanistic distinction between these treatment modalities, with implications for the breadth and durability of therapeutic response.

Comparative specificity to rTMS reveals fundamental differences in how these interventions modulate neural circuits, with ECT producing global rewiring while rTMS achieves more targeted circuit modulation. The focal nature of magnetic stimulation allows for selective targeting of specific cortical regions and their associated networks, whereas ECT's mechanism inherently involves the recruitment of widespread neural circuits through seizure propagation. This distinction has important implications for understanding which clinical populations and symptom profiles may respond optimally to each intervention, with ECT potentially benefiting from its global action in cases of severe, treatment-refractory illness involving distributed network dysfunction.

Whether ECT produces similar spine remodeling and pruning patterns to rTMS remains an important empirical question with significant theoretical implications. The available evidence suggests that while both interventions can induce structural plastic changes at the level of dendritic spines, the global seizure in ECT likely produces more widespread and potentially less selective remodeling compared to the more targeted spine changes associated with focal rTMS. The anticonvulsant effects observed following both treatments indicate shared final common pathways involving normalization of aberrant neural excitability, though the route by which these effects are achieved differs substantially between modalities 00138-x). Further research directly comparing spine-level changes between ECT and rTMS within equivalent experimental frameworks would substantially advance understanding of their relative plastic mechanisms and inform optimal clinical deployment of these neuromodulation strategies.

## Psychedelic-Induced Structural Plasticity: 5-HT2A Receptor Mechanisms and Dendritic Remodeling

The serotonergic psychedelic class of compounds, encompassing psilocybin, lysergic acid diethylamide (LSD), and N,N-dimethyltryptamine (DMT), exerts their profound psychopharmacological effects primarily through activation of the 5-hydroxytryptamine 2A (5-HT2A) receptor, a Gq-coupled metabotropic receptor densely expressed in cortical pyramidal neurons ([Nichols 2016](https://doi.org/10.1124/pr.115.011478)). The structural elucidation of the 5-HT2A receptor in complex with psychedelic ligands has revealed distinct conformational states stabilized by these compounds, providing mechanistic insight into how hallucinogenic and non-hallucinogenic analogs differentially engage downstream signaling cascades ([Kim et al. 2020](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cell.2020.08.024)). Notably, the pharmacology of 5-HT2A receptors exhibits functional selectivity, wherein different ligands preferentially activate specific signaling pathways independent of G protein coupling, a property that may underlie the dissociation between hallucinogenic potential and therapeutic efficacy observed in recent studies ([Cummins et al. 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.pharmr.2025.100059)).

The seminal work by Ly and colleagues demonstrated that psychedelics promote both structural and functional neural plasticity in vitro and in vivo, with these effects requiring 5-HT2A receptor activation and resulting in increased dendritic spine density, a marker of spinogenesis ([Ly et al. 2018](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.celrep.2018.05.022)). This structural remodeling appears to be mediated through brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF) signaling, as psychedelics upregulate BDNF expression and activate downstream mTOR-dependent translational pathways that facilitate synaptic protein synthesis necessary for the formation of new dendritic spines ([Olson 2022](https://doi.org/10.1021/acs.biochem.1c00812)). The prolonged epigenomic and synaptic plasticity alterations observed following single exposure to psychedelics in mice suggest that these structural changes may underlie the long-lasting behavioral effects of psychedelic compounds ([de la Fuente Revenga et al. 2021](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.celrep.2021.109836)).

Recent investigations have further refined our understanding of the cellular specificity required for psychedelic-induced plasticity. Shao and colleagues demonstrated that psilocybin's enduring actions necessitate both 5-HT2A receptor activation and intact pyramidal cell function, revealing that the network-level rewiring observed after psychedelic administration depends on specific cortical neuron populations ([Shao et al. 2025](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-025-08813-6)). This specificity aligns with earlier work demonstrating that hallucinogens recruit specific cortical 5-HT2A receptor-mediated signaling pathways to affect behavior, indicating that the downstream molecular cascades engaged by psychedelic-occupied 5-HT2A receptors are not uniform across all neuronal subtypes ([González-Maeso et al. 2007](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.neuron.2007.01.008)).

The distinction between hallucinogenic and non-hallucinogenic 5-HT2A agonists has become increasingly important for understanding the therapeutic potential of these compounds. The identification of non-hallucinogenic psychedelic analogs that retain therapeutic-like behavioral and synaptic actions suggests that the structural plasticity mechanisms can be dissociated from the subjective psychedelic experience ([Cameron et al. 2021](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-020-3008-z)). Structure-based discovery efforts have successfully generated nonhallucinogenic psychedelic analogs that maintain beneficial plasticity mechanisms while lacking hallucinogenic potential, representing a promising avenue for drug development ([Cao et al. 2022](https://doi.org/10.1126/science.abl8615)). However, conflicting evidence exists regarding the necessity of 5-HT2A receptor activation for psychedelic-induced antidepressant-like effects, as one study demonstrated that psilocybin's behavioral and synaptic actions in mice are independent of 5-HT2R activation ([Hesselgrave et al. 2021](https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.2022489118)), highlighting the complexity of psychedelic pharmacology and the potential involvement of additional receptor targets.

The polypharmacology of psychedelics reveals multiple targets beyond 5-HT2A that may contribute to their plasticity-inducing properties, including 5-HT2C receptors, trace amine-associated receptors, and β-adrenergic receptors ([Jain et al. 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.neuron.2025.06.012)). This broader target engagement may explain the breadth of psychedelic-induced plasticity compared to more targeted neuromodulation approaches. Functional neuroimaging in humans has provided evidence for network-level effects consistent with this plasticity hypothesis, demonstrating that psilocybin therapy increases global brain integration in depressed patients, suggesting that psychedelic-induced structural changes at the synaptic level manifest as large-scale functional reorganization of brain networks ([Daws et al. 2022](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41591-022-01744-z)).

When comparing the plasticity mechanisms of psychedelics to repetitive transcranial magnetic stimulation (rTMS), a FDA-approved neuromodulation therapy for depression, the fundamental distinction lies in the spatial precision versus breadth of effect. Psychedelics, through their widespread 5-HT2A receptor activation across cortical and subcortical regions, induce distributed structural remodeling including spinogenesis, BDNF-mediated plasticity, and network-level rewiring throughout the brain, representing a broad-spectrum approach to neural circuit reorganization ([Hatzipantelis and Olson 2024](https://doi.org/10.1146/annurev-physiol-042022-020923)). In contrast, rTMS delivers targeted electromagnetic stimulation to specific cortical regions, inducing more localized plasticity effects through mechanisms including modulation of glutamatergic signaling, enhancement of BDNF expression, and normalization of hypoactive frontal networks in depression ([Chen et al. 2023](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.phrs.2023.106837)). While psychedelics engage multiple molecular targets simultaneously to promote global brain network flexibility and structural plasticity, rTMS offers spatial precision allowing targeted modulation of dysfunctional circuits, with each approach representing distinct therapeutic philosophies for addressing depression and related disorders through plasticity modulation.

## The Precision of rTMS: Frequency-Dependent Directionality and Circuit-Specific Rewiring

The Precision of rTMS: Frequency-Dependent Directionality and Circuit-Specific Rewiring

Repetitive transcranial magnetic stimulation distinguishes itself among neuromodulatory interventions through its capacity to induce directional plasticity—enabling clinicians and researchers to selectively potentiate or suppress cortical excitability with remarkable temporal and spatial precision. This directional control emerges from the sophisticated interplay between stimulation parameters, particularly frequency and temporal patterning, which can be calibrated to mimic endogenous synaptic plasticity mechanisms. The fundamental principle underlying rTMS plasticity relies on frequency-dependent induction of long-term potentiation (LTP)-like and long-term depression (LTD)-like processes, whereby high-frequency stimulation protocols facilitate excitatory synaptic strengthening while low-frequency paradigms suppress synaptic efficacy ([Antal et al. 2022](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cnp.2022.05.002)). Contemporary computational models have substantially advanced our understanding of these mechanisms, demonstrating that the observed plasticity effects arise from activity-dependent modifications in synaptic strength mediated through NMDA receptor activation and intracellular signaling cascades that parallel naturally occurring hippocampal LTP and LTD phenomena ([Wilson et al. 2018](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.clinph.2018.03.018)).

The sophistication of rTMS-induced plasticity extends substantially beyond simple frequency categorization through the development of patterned stimulation protocols, most notably theta burst stimulation (TBS). Originally derived from observations of hippocampal theta rhythm-dependent plasticity, TBS delivers bursts of high-frequency stimuli at theta frequencies, thereby recapitulating physiologically relevant temporal dynamics ([Cárdenas-Morales et al. 2010](https://doi.org/10.1007/s10548-009-0084-7)). The critical insight driving TBS application concerns the polarity reversal observed between continuous and intermittent delivery patterns: continuous TBS (cTBS) predominantly suppresses motor cortex excitability while intermittent TBS (iTBS) produces facilitation, effects that persist beyond the stimulation period and exhibit characteristics consistent with LTP and LTD-like synaptic modification respectively ([Suppa et al. 2016](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.brs.2016.01.006)). This pattern-dependent directionality has been extensively replicated across populations including healthy volunteers and patients with neurological and psychiatric conditions, though considerable interindividual variability in responsiveness has been documented and may relate to genetic factors, age-dependent plasticity capacity, and baseline cortical excitability states ([Guerra et al. 2020](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.neulet.2017.12.058)).

The therapeutic exploitation of TBS paradigms has received particular attention in psychiatric applications, with systematic reviews confirming that both iTBS and cTBS can modulate the motor cortex excitability in patients with schizophrenia, though the direction and magnitude of effects may differ from healthy populations due to underlying neuroplasticity alterations characteristic of the disorder ([Poorganji et al. 2023](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.schres.2023.10.005)). Neuroimaging investigations employing functional magnetic resonance imaging have corroborated these findings by demonstrating that TBS produces distributed changes in activation patterns extending beyond the stimulated region, suggesting that the induced plasticity propagates through anatomically connected networks in a manner that can be predicted from known cortico-subcortical connectivity ([Kirkovski et al. 2023](https://doi.org/10.1007/s00429-023-02634-x)). The cellular and molecular mechanisms mediating these effects involve modifications in GABAergic inhibition, glutamatergic receptor, and brain trafficking-derived neurotrophic factor signaling, collectively supporting the notion that TBS engages fundamental plasticity machinery that underlies experience-dependent cortical reorganization ([Sharbafshaaer et al. 2024](https://doi.org/10.3390/biomedicines12112506)).

The spatial precision of rTMS constitutes perhaps its most distinctive advantage relative to other neuromodulatory approaches. Unlike electroconvulsive therapy, which induces generalized seizures affecting the entire brain and producing widespread cognitive effects, rTMS can target specific cortical regions with a spatial resolution of approximately one to two centimeters when applied using figure-of-eight coils, and even greater specificity can be achieved through novel approaches such as focused ultrasound-guided targeting that permit depth-specific modulation within the primary motor cortex ([Bao et al. 2024](https://doi.org/10.1113/JP285613)). This focality enables clinicians to selectively modulate circuits implicated in specific symptom domains—for instance, targeting the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex for depressive symptom improvement or the parietal cortex for neuropathic pain relief—rather than producing global alterations in brain function. The capacity for circuit-specific targeting is particularly relevant when considering the mechanistic alternatives offered by psychedelic compounds, which exert their effects through widespread 5-HT2A receptor activation across distributed cortical and subcortical networks, producing network-level changes that, while therapeutically promising, lack the anatomical specificity achievable with rTMS ([Sveva et al. 2025](https://doi.org/10.3390/cells14241996)).

The directional precision afforded by rTMS parameter selection creates possibilities for therapeutic intervention that remain distinct from both convulsive and pharmacological approaches. While ECT remains the most potent intervention for treatment-resistant depression, its mechanism necessarily involves generalized seizure activity and associated cognitive side effects that limit its applicability, whereas rTMS permits sustained modulation of targeted circuits without inducing convulsions or requiring anesthesia. Similarly, psychedelic-assisted therapies operate through fundamentally different principles, engaging serotoninergic transmission to produce acute alterations in consciousness and distributed network dynamics that subsequently facilitate psychological processing during psychotherapy sessions; these mechanisms complement rather than replace the circuit-specific, directionally controlled plasticity achievable with rTMS. The capacity to choose between excitatory and inhibitory plasticity states depending on clinical objectives—employing high-frequency or intermittent TBS to enhance deficient cortical processing in conditions such as depression or cognitive impairment, or continuous TBS to suppress hyperactive circuits in conditions such as tinnitus or obsessive-compulsive disorder—represents a level of mechanistic control unavailable through pharmacotherapy or global neuromodulatory approaches. Future advances in targeting methodology, including robotic navigation, individual functional mapping, and closed-loop protocols responsive to real-time neurophysiological markers, promise to further enhance the precision with which rTMS can orchestrate circuit-specific rewiring in clinical practice.

## ProBDNF/TrkB Signaling in rTMS: Molecular Mechanisms of Synaptic Refinement

The balance between proBDNF and mature BDNF represents a fundamental molecular switch governing synaptic plasticity, where the protease-mediated cleavage of proBDNF determines whether neural circuits undergo refinement or consolidation. ProBDNF, secreted as a precursor molecule, requires proteolytic processing by extracellular proteases including matrix metalloproteinase-9 (MMP-9) and tissue plasminogen activator (tPA) to generate the mature BDNF form capable of TrkB receptor activation ([Costa et al. 2018](https://doi.org/10.1007/s12035-017-0505-7)). This enzymatic conversion creates a temporally and spatially regulated system where the same neurotrophin precursor can exert opposing effects on neuronal connectivity depending on the local proteolytic environment.

The functional consequences of this dichotomy stem from the distinct receptor systems engaged by each neurotrophin form. Mature BDNF binds preferentially to TrkB receptors, initiating downstream signaling cascades that promote neuronal survival, dendritic arborization, and long-term potentiation ([Leal et al. 2017](https://doi.org/10.1016/bs.vh.2016.10.004)). In contrast, proBDNF signals through the p75NTR receptor in conjunction with sortilin co-receptors, activating caspase-dependent apoptotic pathways and facilitating synaptic pruning during development and pathological states ([Mitrovic et al. 2025](https://doi.org/10.3390/ijms26104926)). The significance of maintaining proper proBDNF-to-mature BDNF conversion is underscored by evidence that inhibiting this cleavage process produces autism spectrum disorder-like phenotypes in vivo, demonstrating that the proBDNF/p75NTR signaling axis is not merely a developmental remnant but remains functionally relevant in adult neural circuits ([Yang et al. 2024](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41380-024-02595-5)).

Astrocytes play a critical role in modulating this equilibrium through their secretion of both neurotrophin forms and the proteases responsible for conversion. Astrocytic release of proBDNF and mature BDNF is differentially regulated by neuronal activity and glucocorticoid signaling, positioning these glial cells as key arbiters of the neurotrophin balance in the tripartite synapse ([Albini et al. 2023](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.neures.2023.02.001)). Under conditions of chronic stress, the proBDNF/p75NTR/sortilin pathway becomes upregulated while mature BDNF/TrkB signaling is downregulated, creating a shift toward synaptic elimination that contributes to depressive phenotypes ([Yang et al. 2020](https://doi.org/10.1007/s12640-019-00101-2)). This dysregulation can be corrected by antidepressant treatments, which restore the proBDNF/BDNF ratio toward physiological norms.

Repetitive transcranial magnetic stimulation exerts its therapeutic effects through a mechanism that appears to specifically target this neurotrophin equilibrium, though the precise molecular details remain under active investigation. The electromagnetic fields induced by rTMS are thought to activate voltage-gated calcium channels and promote neuronal firing patterns that favor activity-dependent secretion of mature BDNF over proBDNF. This contrasts with electroconvulsive therapy, which induces generalized seizures resulting in more widespread neurotrophin release and concurrent activation of both TrkB and p75NTR pathways. The broader plasticity enhancement observed with ECT likely reflects the simultaneous engagement of multiple neurotrophin signaling cascades across wider neural networks, whereas rTMS may achieve more targeted synaptic refinement through its focal application and frequency-dependent effects on the proBDNF/BDNF cleavage machinery.

Psychedelic compounds represent another class of plasticity-modulating interventions that differ substantially from both rTMS and ECT in their mechanistic underpinnings. Psychedelic-induced plasticity appears to be mediated primarily through 5-HT2A receptor activation and downstream mTOR signaling, leading to rapid increases in mature BDNF expression and spine density across distributed cortical networks ([Qiu et al. 2025](https://doi.org/10.1021/acschemneuro.5c00193)). While this results in profound structural plasticity, the relative contribution of proBDNF versus mature BDNF signaling to psychedelic-induced effects remains less characterized. The distinction between these intervention modalities may ultimately prove therapeutically meaningful: rTMS may offer refined, circuit-specific modulation of synaptic architecture through careful manipulation of the proBDNF/TrkB balance, while ECT and psychedelics produce more generalized plasticity states that, while effective for treatment-resistant depression, carry different side effect profiles and mechanistic implications for long-term neural circuit remodeling.

## Clinical Implications of Differential Rewiring Mechanisms

The translation of differential rewiring mechanisms into clinical practice represents a paradigm shift in psychiatric treatment, moving from symptom-based algorithms toward circuit-informed personalization. The recognition that depression manifests through distinct neurobiological substrates—particularly involving dysfunction within prefrontal cortical circuits and their downstream connectivity to subcortical structures—has profound implications for treatment selection and targeting strategies ([Cash et al. 2021](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.biopsych.2020.05.033)).

Treatment selection criteria based on circuit-level dysfunction have emerged as a critical advancement in repetitive transcranial magnetic stimulation (rTMS) for depression. Functional connectivity between the left dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (DLPFC) and the striatum has demonstrated predictive validity for treatment response, such that patients exhibiting stronger baseline connectivity between these regions show superior antidepressant outcomes following rTMS ([Avissar et al. 2017](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.brs.2017.07.002)). Similarly, connectivity patterns involving the anterior cingulate cortex (ACC) reliably predict outcome at follow-up assessments, suggesting that pre-treatment characterization of frontolimbic circuitry can inform patient selection ([Ge et al. 2020](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.brs.2019.10.012)). These findings indicate that the mechanistic heterogeneity within the broad diagnostic category of major depressive disorder maps onto differential treatment responsiveness, with circuit-specific profiles determining which patients are most likely to benefit from neuromodulation interventions.

The identification of distinct symptom cluster responses to rTMS further underscores the importance of mechanism-informed treatment selection. Research examining differential symptom trajectories has revealed that specific depressive manifestations—such as anhedonia versus general melancholic features—respond preferentially to different stimulation targets, reflecting the underlying neuroanatomy of these symptom dimensions ([Downar et al. 2014](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.biopsych.2013.10.026)). Patients with prominent anhedonic symptoms demonstrate distinct patterns of reward-circuit connectivity that differentiate treatment responders from nonresponders, suggesting that this particular symptom dimension may require targeting of medial prefrontal regions rather than the standard lateral DLPFC site ([Downar et al. 2014](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.biopsych.2013.10.026)). These observations support a transdiagnostic approach to circuit-based treatment selection, wherein specific psychiatric symptoms are mapped onto measurable neural circuitry dysfunction rather than categorical diagnoses alone.

The cognitive side effect profile of rTMS represents a critical consideration in clinical implementation, particularly given the central role of prefrontal cortical function in cognition. The dorsolateral prefrontal cortex, the primary target of conventional high-frequency rTMS protocols, demonstrates particular vulnerability to stress and inflammation through mechanisms affecting layer III pyramidal cell circuits that subserve working memory and cognitive control ([Joyce et al. 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.biopsych.2024.06.016)). This vulnerability raises the theoretical concern that stimulation of already-compromised prefrontal circuits could exacerbate existing cognitive deficits in certain patient populations. However, the evidence base regarding clinically significant cognitive adverse effects remains heterogeneous, with studies demonstrating that proper targeting and individualized parameter selection can mitigate potential cognitive burden while maintaining antidepressant efficacy ([Schiena et al. 2020](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jad.2020.02.006)). The relationship between rTMS and cognition appears to be state-dependent, such that baseline prefrontal function may determine whether stimulation produces neutral, beneficial, or adverse cognitive outcomes ([Schiena et al. 2020](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jad.2020.02.006)).

The rationale for precision targeting of specific circuit dysfunction extends beyond depression to other psychiatric conditions characterized by prefrontal pathology. The recognition that disorders including schizophrenia, depression, long COVID, and Alzheimer's disease all involve DLPFC dysfunction—often with common cellular mechanisms affecting layer III circuitry—suggests that circuit-informed neuromodulation may have transdiagnostic applicability ([Joyce et al. 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.biopsych.2024.06.016)). The extensive top-down projections from the DLPFC to posterior association cortices for attention regulation and to the subgenual cingulate for emotional regulation provide neuroanatomical justification for targeting this region across multiple psychiatric presentations ([Joyce et al. 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.biopsych.2024.06.016)).

Advanced neuroimaging approaches have enabled unprecedented precision in spatial targeting, moving beyond anatomically based landmark methods toward functionally individualized navigation. The use of functional connectivity-based targeting allows clinicians to identify the optimal cortical site within the broader DLPFC region that demonstrates the strongest anticorrelation with regions implicated in depression pathophysiology, such as the subgenual anterior cingulate ([Gogulski et al. 2023](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.bpsc.2022.10.006)). This approach has demonstrated superior efficacy compared to standard targeting methods, as the individually determined functional target more reliably engages the frontolimbic networks thought to underlie antidepressant response ([Klooster et al. 2022](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.bpsc.2021.11.004)). Furthermore, large-scale structural network changes observed following successful rTMS treatment correlate with clinical response, providing evidence that the therapeutic mechanism involves actual rewiring of distributed brain networks rather than mere transient modulation ([Nestor et al. 2022](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41386-021-01256-3)).

Emerging technologies in coil design and orientation offer additional avenues for refining targeting precision. The angle of the TMS coil relative to cortical anatomy influences the direction of induced electric fields and consequently which neuronal populations are activated, with implications for both efficacy and side effect profiles ([Cerins et al. 2024](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.bpsc.2024.04.018)). Contemporary approaches also incorporate multimodal neuroimaging data, including structural MRI, functional connectivity, and neurophysiological measures, to develop comprehensive personalized treatment protocols that account for individual differences in anatomy, connectivity, and physiology ([Klooster et al. 2022](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.bpsc.2021.11.004)).

The integration of closed-loop and adaptive stimulation paradigms represents the frontier of precision neuromodulation, enabling real-time adjustment of stimulation parameters based on ongoing neural activity ([Zrenner and Ziemann 2024](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.biopsych.2023.09.014)). Such approaches address the fundamental limitation of static treatment protocols by accommodating the dynamic nature of brain states and network fluctuations. Although these technologies remain primarily in the research domain, they point toward a future in which treatment delivery is continuously optimized based on circuit-level feedback.

In summary, the translation of differential rewiring mechanisms into clinical practice requires a multi-dimensional approach encompassing circuit-based patient selection, careful attention to cognitive safety profiles, and increasingly precise targeting methodologies. The evidence supports that functional neuroimaging-informed targeting improves treatment outcomes compared to conventional approaches, while the characterization of specific symptom-circuit relationships enables more mechanism-informed treatment selection. Continued refinement of these approaches, incorporating emerging technologies and larger validation studies, will be essential for realizing the full potential of precision neuromodulation in psychiatric practice.
