# Applying the "disrupt-potentiate-rewire" framework of electroconvulsive therapy to the mechanism of action of selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors in major depressive disorder

## Introduction: from seizures to serotonin through a unifying lens

The mechanistic understanding of antidepressant treatments has undergone a profound transformation over the past two decades, shifting from simple neurochemical deficit models toward integrated circuit-level and plasticity-based frameworks. A particularly compelling synthesis was advanced by Ousdal and colleagues (2022), who proposed a three-phase "disrupt, potentiate, and rewire" model to account for the neurobiological effects of electroconvulsive therapy (ECT) as observed through magnetic resonance imaging (Ousdal et al., 2022, *Biological Psychiatry*, 91(6), 540–549; DOI: 10.1016/j.biopsych.2021.05.023). In this framework, each ECT session acutely disrupts existing neural network configurations through the induced seizure, as evidenced by transient reductions in N-acetylaspartate, decreased frontal cortical connectivity, and post-ictal cognitive disruption. This disruption triggers a compensatory upregulation of neuroplasticity mechanisms—including neurotrophic factor expression, neurogenesis, and synaptogenesis—that temporarily potentiates the brain's capacity for structural and functional remodeling. Finally, the combination of disrupted maladaptive circuits and enhanced plasticity enables the rewiring of neural networks implicated in depressive symptomatology, including normalization of default mode network (DMN) hyperconnectivity and reconfiguration of frontolimbic circuits.

The elegance of this tripartite model lies in its sequential logic: disruption creates the conditions for change, potentiation provides the biological machinery for change, and rewiring represents the therapeutic outcome. Although the model was developed to explain ECT—arguably the most dramatic somatic intervention in psychiatry—the present essay examines whether this same framework can be meaningfully applied to selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs), the most widely prescribed class of antidepressants worldwide. At first glance, the comparison may seem strained: ECT produces immediate seizure-induced neural perturbation, whereas SSRIs exert their effects through gradual modulation of serotonergic neurotransmission. Yet converging evidence from functional neuroimaging, molecular neuroscience, and cognitive psychology suggests that SSRIs may indeed engage an analogous sequence of disruption, potentiation, and rewiring—albeit on a markedly different timescale and with substantially less dramatic acute perturbation. This essay reviews the evidence supporting each phase of this transposed framework, compares the temporal dynamics between the two treatments, and critically evaluates the limitations and implications of extending the Ousdal model beyond ECT.

## Phase I: early serotonergic perturbation as network disruption

The first phase of the Ousdal framework posits that therapeutic benefit requires an initial disruption of existing, pathological neural network configurations. In ECT, this disruption is unmistakable: the generalized seizure transiently reduces functional connectivity in frontal cortex (Perrin et al., 2012, *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences*, 109(14), 5464–5468; DOI: 10.1073/pnas.1117206109), decreases neuronal integrity markers such as NAA, and produces well-documented cognitive side effects. For SSRIs, the evidence for an analogous disruption phase is subtler but nonetheless substantial.

Acute SSRI administration produces an immediate surge in synaptic serotonin concentration through blockade of the serotonin transporter. Paradoxically, however, this initial increase activates somatodendritic 5-HT₁A autoreceptors on raphe neurons, triggering negative feedback that transiently *reduces* serotonergic firing and net serotonin release in terminal projection areas during the first one to two weeks of treatment. This neurochemical destabilization—a period during which the serotonergic system is effectively perturbed from its established equilibrium—constitutes a plausible molecular substrate for the disruption phase. Only after chronic administration and autoreceptor desensitization does enhanced serotonergic transmission become established in cortical and limbic projection regions.

The functional neuroimaging literature provides compelling evidence that this serotonergic perturbation translates into measurable network-level disruption. Van de Ven and colleagues (2013) demonstrated that a single dose of escitalopram in healthy volunteers decreased pairwise functional connectivity within the DMN, including connections between the anterior and posterior cingulate cortex, hippocampal formation, and lateral parietal regions (*PLoS ONE*, 8(6), e68355; DOI: 10.1371/journal.pone.0068355). Klaassens and colleagues (2015) similarly showed that a single serotonergic dose produced **widespread changes in functional connectivity** across multiple resting-state networks (*NeuroImage*, 122, 440–450; DOI: 10.1016/j.neuroimage.2015.08.012). These findings indicate that acute serotonergic modulation rapidly and broadly perturbs the intrinsic organization of large-scale brain networks. Van Wingen and colleagues (2014) extended this observation, showing that two weeks of antidepressant administration reduced connectivity within the DMN and uncoupled the medial prefrontal cortex from the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex in healthy individuals (*NeuroImage*, 88, 47–53; DOI: 10.1016/j.neuroimage.2013.11.022), suggesting disruption of established network configurations even in the non-depressed brain. In patients with major depressive disorder, Dutta and colleagues (2019) demonstrated that acute intravenous citalopram rapidly reconfigured DMN intra-network connectivity, modifying the aberrant inclusion of the amygdala within the DMN that characterizes the depressed state (*Translational Psychiatry*, 9, 116; DOI: 10.1038/s41398-019-0447-0).

Perhaps the most striking evidence for early SSRI-induced disruption comes from studies of amygdala reactivity. In a landmark investigation, Godlewska and colleagues (2012) randomized unmedicated depressed patients to seven days of escitalopram or placebo and found that **SSRI treatment normalized amygdala hyperactivity to fearful faces** within one week—critically, in the complete absence of any clinical mood improvement (*Psychological Medicine*, 42(12), 2609–2617; DOI: 10.1017/S0033291712000591). Murphy and colleagues (2009) demonstrated that this effect occurs with remarkable speed: a single 20-mg dose of citalopram significantly reduced amygdala responses to fearful faces in healthy volunteers within three hours of administration (*British Journal of Psychiatry*, 194(6), 535–540; DOI: 10.1192/bjp.bp.108.056093). These rapid neural changes precede any alteration in subjective mood and represent a genuine disruption of established patterns of emotional reactivity.

The clinical phenomenology of early SSRI treatment provides further support for a disruption phase. The jitteriness/anxiety syndrome—characterized by increased anxiety, agitation, insomnia, and irritability in the first days of treatment—affects between 4% and 65% of patients initiating antidepressant therapy, with approximately half of cases emerging within three days and two-thirds within one week (Sinclair et al., 2009, *British Journal of Psychiatry*, 194(6), 483–490; DOI: 10.1192/bjp.bp.107.048371). Similarly, McCabe and colleagues (2010) demonstrated that seven days of citalopram diminished neural processing of both rewarding and aversive stimuli in the ventral striatum and orbitofrontal cortex (*Biological Psychiatry*, 67(5), 439–445; DOI: 10.1016/j.biopsych.2009.11.001), a finding that may explain the emotional blunting reported by a substantial proportion of SSRI-treated patients. These phenomena—paradoxical increases in anxiety and a transient flattening of emotional valence processing—are consistent with an initial destabilization of existing affective processing networks.

It is essential, however, to acknowledge the quantitative difference between SSRI-induced and ECT-induced disruption. ECT produces a generalized seizure that acutely reduces neuronal integrity markers, causes measurable cognitive impairment, and dramatically alters connectivity across cortical and subcortical structures within minutes. SSRI-induced disruption, by contrast, is gradual, partial, and pharmacologically mediated rather than electrically induced. The disruption is more akin to a selective perturbation of serotonin-modulated circuits than a global neural reset. This difference in magnitude and specificity may contribute to the markedly different timescales of therapeutic response—days to weeks for ECT versus weeks to months for SSRIs—and suggests that the SSRI disruption phase is better understood as a sustained, low-grade destabilization rather than the acute, high-intensity perturbation characteristic of ECT.

## Phase II: serotonin as a gate for neuroplasticity enhancement

The second phase of the Ousdal framework proposes that neural disruption triggers a compensatory upregulation of neuroplasticity. For ECT, this is evidenced by robust hippocampal volume increases, elevated serum BDNF, and increased neurogenesis in animal models. The evidence that SSRIs similarly enhance neuroplastic potential is now extensive and constitutes one of the most influential reconceptualizations in modern psychopharmacology: the neuroplasticity hypothesis of antidepressant action.

The foundational observation was reported by Nibuya, Morinobu, and Duman (1995), who demonstrated that chronic, but not acute, administration of several antidepressant classes—including the SSRI sertraline—significantly increased BDNF mRNA and TrkB mRNA expression in the rat hippocampus (*Journal of Neuroscience*, 15(11), 7539–7547; DOI: 10.1523/JNEUROSCI.15-11-07539.1995). Non-antidepressant psychotropic agents did not produce this effect. This discovery initiated what Duman and colleagues subsequently formalized as the neurotrophic model of depression (Duman et al., 1997, *Archives of General Psychiatry*, 54(7), 597–606; DOI: 10.1001/archpsyc.1997.01830190015002; Duman & Monteggia, 2006, *Biological Psychiatry*, 59(12), 1116–1127; DOI: 10.1016/j.biopsych.2006.02.013), proposing that stress-induced downregulation of neurotrophic factors contributes to neuronal atrophy in the hippocampus and prefrontal cortex, while antidepressants reverse these deficits through BDNF-mediated trophic signaling. Clinical meta-analyses have confirmed that **SSRI treatment increases peripheral BDNF levels** in depressed patients (Zhou et al., 2017, *PLoS ONE*, 12(2), e0172270; DOI: 10.1371/journal.pone.0172270), with sertraline showing particularly robust early BDNF elevation.

A parallel line of evidence established hippocampal neurogenesis as a cellular substrate of antidepressant action. Malberg and colleagues (2000) demonstrated that chronic antidepressant treatment, including fluoxetine, significantly increased the proliferation of new neurons in the adult rat dentate gyrus, with a time course matching the therapeutic lag of antidepressant response (*Journal of Neuroscience*, 20(24), 9104–9110; DOI: 10.1523/JNEUROSCI.20-24-09104.2000). Santarelli and colleagues (2003) provided the critical causal evidence: hippocampal irradiation that ablated neurogenesis abolished the behavioral effects of both fluoxetine and imipramine in mice, establishing that **hippocampal neurogenesis is required for at least some antidepressant behavioral effects** (*Science*, 301(5634), 805–809; DOI: 10.1126/science.1083328). Crucially, human postmortem studies by Boldrini and colleagues (2009) confirmed that SSRI-treated patients with major depression had significantly more neural progenitor cells and larger dentate gyrus volume compared with both untreated depressed patients and healthy controls (*Neuropsychopharmacology*, 34(11), 2376–2389; DOI: 10.1038/npp.2009.75), though subsequent work by David and colleagues (2009) demonstrated that fluoxetine also exerts neurogenesis-independent effects (*Neuron*, 62(4), 479–493; DOI: 10.1016/j.neuron.2009.04.017), indicating that neurogenesis is one of several plasticity mechanisms contributing to therapeutic response.

At the synaptic level, SSRIs promote dendritic remodeling and spine density changes. Hajszan and colleagues (2005) showed that even short-term fluoxetine treatment (five days) induced a robust increase in pyramidal cell dendritic spine synapse density in hippocampal CA1 (*European Journal of Neuroscience*, 21(5), 1299–1303; DOI: 10.1111/j.1460-9568.2005.03968.x). Bessa and colleagues (2009) demonstrated that the mood-improving actions of antidepressants correlate with dendritic reorganization even independently of neurogenesis (*Molecular Psychiatry*, 14(8), 764–773; DOI: 10.1038/mp.2008.119), while Duman and Aghajanian (2012) articulated the broader synaptogenic hypothesis—that depression reflects reduced synaptic connections in prefrontal cortex and hippocampus, and that antidepressants reverse these deficits (*Science*, 338(6103), 68–72; DOI: 10.1126/science.1222939). Human evidence for SSRI-enhanced plasticity comes from transcranial direct current stimulation studies: Kuo and colleagues (2016) demonstrated that chronic citalopram administration in healthy volunteers increased and prolonged LTP-like cortical plasticity for over 24 hours through NMDA-receptor-dependent mechanisms (*Neuropsychopharmacology*, 41(5), 1223–1230; DOI: 10.1038/npp.2015.270), providing direct human evidence that chronic serotonergic enhancement strengthens glutamatergic plasticity.

Perhaps the most conceptually transformative contribution has been the recognition that serotonin functions not as a direct mood effector but as a **gating mechanism for plasticity**. Maya Vetencourt and colleagues (2008) demonstrated that chronic fluoxetine reinstated ocular dominance plasticity in the adult rat visual cortex—a form of developmental critical-period plasticity normally restricted to early life—and promoted recovery of visual function in amblyopic animals (*Science*, 320(5874), 385–388; DOI: 10.1126/science.1150516). This effect was accompanied by reduced intracortical GABAergic inhibition and increased BDNF expression, and was blocked by the GABA-potentiating agent diazepam. Karpova and colleagues (2011) extended this principle to fear memory circuits, showing that chronic fluoxetine combined with extinction training permanently erased conditioned fear responses in mice, while neither treatment alone was effective (*Science*, 334(6063), 1731–1734; DOI: 10.1126/science.1214592). The effect required intact BDNF signaling, demonstrating that fluoxetine creates a permissive plasticity state that must be combined with appropriate experiential input to achieve lasting circuit reorganization.

These findings converge in the neuroplasticity hypothesis formalized by Castrén and colleagues, who proposed that antidepressants reactivate a developmental-like state of enhanced plasticity—termed "iPlasticity" (induced juvenile-like plasticity)—in the adult brain, allowing environmental input and therapeutic experiences to reshape dysfunctional neural networks (Castrén, 2005, *Nature Reviews Neuroscience*, 6(3), 241–246; DOI: 10.1038/nrn1629; Castrén & Hen, 2013, *Trends in Neurosciences*, 36(5), 259–267; DOI: 10.1016/j.tins.2012.12.010; Castrén, 2013, *JAMA Psychiatry*, 70(9), 983–989; DOI: 10.1001/jamapsychiatry.2013.1). A remarkable molecular discovery by Casarotto and colleagues (2021) revealed that antidepressant drugs, including fluoxetine, directly bind to the transmembrane domain of TrkB neurotrophin receptors, promoting BDNF signaling through a mechanism independent of serotonin reuptake inhibition (*Cell*, 184(5), 1299–1313; DOI: 10.1016/j.cell.2021.01.034). This finding suggests that the neuroplasticity-enhancing effects of SSRIs may be mediated, at least in part, by direct engagement of neurotrophic signaling rather than solely through downstream consequences of increased synaptic serotonin.

## Phase III: rewiring of neural circuits through convergent plasticity and experience

The third and final phase of the Ousdal framework—rewiring—represents the therapeutic outcome: the reorganization of maladaptive neural circuits into more adaptive configurations. For ECT, this manifests as normalization of DMN connectivity, reduction of frontal hyperconnectivity, and reconfiguration of frontolimbic circuits. For SSRIs, the evidence for analogous circuit-level reorganization over weeks to months of treatment is substantial, though it unfolds on a considerably slower timescale.

Longitudinal neuroimaging studies consistently demonstrate that SSRI treatment normalizes the hyperconnectivity within the DMN that characterizes the depressed brain. Posner and colleagues (2013) conducted a ten-week double-blind randomized controlled trial in patients with dysthymia and found that **duloxetine, but not placebo, normalized elevated DMN functional connectivity** to levels comparable to healthy controls (*JAMA Psychiatry*, 70(4), 373–382; DOI: 10.1001/jamapsychiatry.2013.455). The large-scale REST-meta-MDD consortium analysis by Yan and colleagues (2019), encompassing over 1,300 patients and 1,128 controls, confirmed that medicated depressed patients showed reduced DMN functional connectivity relative to unmedicated patients, with greater medication benefit correlating with more DMN normalization (*Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences*, 116(18), 9078–9083; DOI: 10.1073/pnas.1900390116). A systematic review by Dichter and colleagues (2015) synthesized the broader literature, finding that antidepressant treatment response is consistently associated with normalization of anomalous resting-state functional networks, particularly increased frontal-limbic and frontal-DMN connectivity (*Journal of Affective Disorders*, 172, 8–17; DOI: 10.1016/j.jad.2014.09.028).

The rewiring of frontolimbic circuits—specifically the strengthening of prefrontal regulatory control over limbic structures—represents perhaps the most functionally significant change during SSRI treatment. In a seminal study, Anand and colleagues (2005) demonstrated that six weeks of sertraline treatment significantly increased functional connectivity between the anterior cingulate cortex and limbic regions including the amygdala, medial thalamus, and pallidostriatum, reversing the corticolimbic disconnection observed in the depressed state (*Neuropsychopharmacology*, 30(7), 1334–1344; DOI: 10.1038/sj.npp.1300725). A subsequent study by the same group revealed a reciprocal relationship: as corticolimbic connectivity increased with treatment, limbic activation to negative stimuli decreased (Anand et al., 2007, *Journal of Neuropsychiatry and Clinical Neurosciences*, 19(3), 274–282; DOI: 10.1176/jnp.2007.19.3.274). These findings suggest that SSRI treatment does not simply attenuate limbic hyperactivation but fundamentally restructures the regulatory relationship between cortical and subcortical mood circuits.

The restoration of cognitive control network function provides additional evidence for circuit rewiring. Fales and colleagues (2009) demonstrated that eight weeks of SSRI treatment **normalized hypoactivity in the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex** during emotional interference processing in depressed patients, such that post-treatment neural activation patterns were indistinguishable from healthy controls (*Journal of Affective Disorders*, 112(1–3), 206–211; DOI: 10.1016/j.jad.2008.04.027). Young and colleagues (2020) showed that SSRI responders exhibited a characteristic recalibration of amygdala responses—increased reactivity to positive stimuli and decreased reactivity to negative stimuli—while non-responders showed no such change (*NeuroImage: Clinical*, 28, 102388; DOI: 10.1016/j.nicl.2020.102388), suggesting that successful treatment requires effective reorganization of amygdala response patterns.

The cognitive neuropsychological model developed by Harmer, Cowen, and colleagues provides a particularly elegant account of how early disruption translates into eventual rewiring. This model proposes that SSRIs produce rapid changes in emotional information processing—specifically, a reduction of negative biases in attention, perception, and memory—within hours to days of initiating treatment, well before any subjective mood improvement (Harmer et al., 2009, *British Journal of Psychiatry*, 195(2), 102–108; DOI: 10.1192/bjp.bp.108.051193; Godlewska & Harmer, 2021, *Psychopharmacology*, 238(5), 1265–1278; DOI: 10.1007/s00213-019-05448-0). Harmer and colleagues (2004) demonstrated that a single dose of an antidepressant increased the recognition of positive facial expressions and enhanced recall of positive emotional material in healthy volunteers (*American Journal of Psychiatry*, 161(7), 1256–1263; DOI: 10.1176/appi.ajp.161.7.1256). Critically, Godlewska and colleagues (2016) showed that the magnitude of these early neural changes—measured at just seven days—predicted clinical response at six weeks, with mediation analyses confirming that this neural effect was independent of initial symptom changes (*Translational Psychiatry*, 6, e957; DOI: 10.1038/tp.2016.130). The therapeutic delay, in this model, reflects the time required for corrected emotional processing biases to translate, through ongoing social and environmental interactions, into relearned emotional associations and ultimately sustained mood improvement.

Harmer, Duman, and Cowen (2017) proposed a crucial synthesis: early changes in emotional processing (the cognitive neuropsychological mechanism) and enhanced neuroplasticity (the neurotrophic mechanism) are not competing explanations but complementary aspects of a unified process (*The Lancet Psychiatry*, 4(5), 409–418; DOI: 10.1016/S2215-0366(17)30015-9). Price and Duman (2020) formalized this integration by modeling neuroplasticity as a multi-level construct spanning molecular, network, cognitive, and behavioral levels (*Molecular Psychiatry*, 25, 530–543; DOI: 10.1038/s41380-019-0615-x). In the language of the Ousdal framework, the early shift in emotional processing bias can be understood as the experiential input that guides the plastic brain—rendered malleable by SSRI-induced potentiation—toward more adaptive circuit configurations. The rewiring phase thus emerges from the synergy of enhanced plasticity and corrected information processing, a formulation strikingly consonant with Castrén's concept of iPlasticity as a permissive, rather than instructive, state (Castrén, 2017, *Molecular Psychiatry*, 22(8), 1085–1095; DOI: 10.1038/mp.2017.61).

Structural brain changes with long-term SSRI use provide evidence for the physical consolidation of rewired circuits. Sheline and colleagues (2003) demonstrated that longer durations of untreated depression were associated with hippocampal volume loss, while time depressed during antidepressant treatment showed no such association, suggesting a neuroprotective effect (*American Journal of Psychiatry*, 160(8), 1516–1518; DOI: 10.1176/appi.ajp.160.8.1516). Bartlett and colleagues (2018), using data from the large EMBARC trial, showed that early-treatment cortical thickening in the rostral anterior cingulate cortex during the first week of sertraline predicted SSRI response at eight weeks (*Neuropsychopharmacology*, 43(11), 2221–2230; DOI: 10.1038/s41386-018-0122-9). Together with the meta-analytic evidence linking depression duration to progressive hippocampal volume reduction (Videbech & Ravnkilde, 2004, *American Journal of Psychiatry*, 161(11), 1957–1966; DOI: 10.1176/appi.ajp.161.11.1957), these findings suggest that SSRIs not only promote functional circuit rewiring but also support the structural remodeling necessary to consolidate and maintain these new configurations.

## Temporal dynamics: a slow burn versus an electric shock

The most obvious distinction between ECT and SSRIs within the disrupt-potentiate-rewire framework is temporal. ECT delivers its disruption in seconds, produces measurable neuroplastic changes within days, and achieves clinical remission within one to two weeks across a typical treatment course of six to twelve sessions. SSRIs, by contrast, initiate a pharmacological perturbation that builds over days as serotonin transporter occupancy increases, produce maximal neuroplastic enhancement over weeks as autoreceptors desensitize and BDNF upregulation matures, and achieve clinical rewiring over four to twelve weeks of continuous treatment. This temporal difference has important implications. The compressed timescale of ECT may explain both its superior efficacy in severe, treatment-resistant depression and its more prominent cognitive side effects—the disruption is sufficiently intense to affect circuits subserving both mood and cognition. The extended timescale of SSRIs may permit greater specificity, with the gradual nature of the disruption allowing more selective engagement of serotonin-modulated circuits while sparing non-serotonergic networks, at the cost of slower therapeutic onset.

Furthermore, in ECT, the three phases are partially overlapping but temporally compressed within each session and across the treatment course: each session disrupts, each interval between sessions permits potentiation, and rewiring accumulates over sessions. For SSRIs, the phases are more sequential and extended: the first one to two weeks are dominated by disruption (serotonergic perturbation, emotional processing bias changes, initial network destabilization), weeks two through six see the maturation of potentiation processes (BDNF upregulation, neurogenesis, synaptic remodeling), and weeks four through twelve witness the emergence of functional and structural rewiring (DMN normalization, frontolimbic reconnection, cognitive control restoration). This temporal separation may explain why the combination of SSRIs with psychotherapy is particularly effective: the potentiation phase creates a window of enhanced plasticity during which therapeutically guided experience—the "environmental input" in Castrén's formulation—can optimally direct circuit reorganization.

## Limitations and caveats in transposing the framework

Despite the compelling parallels, several limitations must be acknowledged when applying the disrupt-potentiate-rewire model to SSRIs. First, the disruption produced by SSRIs is qualitatively different from that of ECT. ECT induces a generalized seizure affecting virtually all brain circuits simultaneously; SSRIs produce a pharmacologically selective perturbation of serotonergic neurotransmission that, while having widespread downstream effects, does not approach the magnitude of ECT-induced disruption. Whether the subtle connectivity changes observed after single SSRI doses represent genuine "disruption" in the same mechanistic sense as ECT's seizure-induced perturbation, or whether they are better described as modulation or adjustment, remains debatable.

Second, the causal relationships between the three phases are more firmly established for ECT than for SSRIs. The Ousdal model explicitly proposes that ECT disruption triggers compensatory neuroplasticity, which in turn enables rewiring. For SSRIs, it is less clear that the initial network perturbation is causally necessary for subsequent plasticity enhancement. The direct binding of antidepressants to TrkB receptors described by Casarotto and colleagues (2021) suggests that SSRIs may enhance neuroplasticity through mechanisms that are partially independent of, rather than triggered by, the initial serotonergic disruption. This raises the possibility that disruption and potentiation proceed in parallel rather than in strict sequence for SSRIs.

Third, the neuroplasticity evidence for SSRIs derives heavily from rodent models, and the translation to human depression remains incomplete. While human postmortem studies (Boldrini et al., 2009) and in vivo structural imaging provide supportive evidence, the direct demonstration of SSRI-induced neurogenesis in living human patients has not yet been achieved. Similarly, the elegant demonstrations of iPlasticity by Maya Vetencourt and colleagues (2008) and Karpova and colleagues (2011) involved visual cortex and fear circuitry in rodents, respectively, and the extrapolation to human mood circuits requires assumptions about cross-species and cross-circuit generalizability.

Fourth, not all SSRI-treated patients respond, and the framework does not adequately account for treatment resistance. If SSRIs disrupt, potentiate, and rewire, non-response could reflect failure at any of the three phases—insufficient disruption of maladaptive networks, inadequate neuroplastic enhancement, or ineffective rewiring despite adequate plasticity. Distinguishing between these possibilities has significant implications for treatment optimization but requires biomarkers that remain largely aspirational. Finally, the framework may inadvertently oversimplify what are likely overlapping, interacting, and non-linear processes by imposing a neat sequential structure. Depression is heterogeneous, and the relative contribution of disruption, potentiation, and rewiring likely varies across patients, depression subtypes, and individual neurobiological profiles.

## Conclusion: toward a transdiagnostic mechanism of antidepressant action

The application of the disrupt-potentiate-rewire framework to SSRIs reveals that these two seemingly disparate treatments may engage fundamentally analogous processes differing primarily in intensity, specificity, and temporal scale. SSRIs produce a pharmacologically mediated disruption of serotonin-modulated neural networks, enhance neuroplastic potential through BDNF-TrkB signaling, neurogenesis, and synaptic remodeling, and ultimately facilitate the rewiring of frontolimbic, default mode, and cognitive control circuits toward more adaptive configurations. The cognitive neuropsychological model of Harmer and Cowen maps naturally onto this framework, with early changes in emotional processing bias representing both a manifestation of the disruption phase and the experiential input that guides plastic circuits toward therapeutic rewiring.

This transposition carries several novel implications. It suggests that the therapeutic delay of SSRIs is not merely a pharmacokinetic inconvenience but a necessary consequence of the slower, more gradual progression through the three mechanistic phases. It provides a neurobiological rationale for combining pharmacotherapy with psychotherapy: the drug provides disruption and potentiation, while therapy provides the structured experiential input required to guide rewiring in a therapeutically beneficial direction. It also suggests that treatment strategies aimed at accelerating any of the three phases—for instance, using psilocybin or ketamine for more dramatic acute disruption, or using direct neurostimulation to augment potentiation—might be usefully understood within this same framework (Duman et al., 2016, *Nature Medicine*, 22(3), 238–249; DOI: 10.1038/nm.4050).

The disrupt-potentiate-rewire model, originally conceived for ECT, may thus represent not merely a treatment-specific mechanism but a more general principle of antidepressant action—a common pathway through which diverse interventions achieve their therapeutic effects by destabilizing maladaptive neural configurations, enhancing the brain's capacity for change, and guiding that change toward more adaptive circuit organization. The challenge for future research lies in testing this hypothesis rigorously through longitudinal, multi-modal neuroimaging studies that track all three phases within the same patients across the full trajectory of SSRI treatment, and in determining whether the framework can predict individual treatment response and guide personalized therapeutic strategies.
